6  Troubleshooting & Evaluation

Published

March 6, 2026

Even with the right formulas, technical hurdles or “nonsensical” results can occur. Use this guide to debug your code and validate your findings.

6.1 Common Technical Errors

If you encounter issues while running your R code, refer to the table below for common symptoms and solutions.

Error Message Likely Cause Solution
Error: object 'p1' not found Variable sequence error. Ensure the code chunk that calculates p1 and p0 is placed above the text or table where you call them.
RR = Inf The “Zero \(p_0\)” problem. Your threshold \(u\) is so high that the event never occurred in the natural world. Increase your sample size (n) or slightly lower your threshold.
Warning: Removed k rows Data out of bounds. Your threshold \(u\) is set outside the range of your actual data points. Check your data range using summary(data).
ImportantA Note on Infinity (Inf)

In attribution science, a Risk Ratio of Inf (Infinity) occurs when \(p_0\) is 0. While this suggests the event was “impossible” without human influence, it is usually a sign that your sample size is too small to capture rare natural events. Try increasing your simulation sample size for more stable results.

6.2 Statistical Sanity Check

Before you publish your results, perform a “Sanity Check” to ensure the logic holds up. If your results look like the examples below, you may need to re-evaluate your model:

  • Is \(p_1\) smaller than \(p_0\)?: In a warming world example, the factual probability should be higher. If \(p_1 < p_0\), check if you accidentally swapped your data labels or if you are studying a variable that should be decreasing (like extreme cold).

  • Is the Risk Ratio (RR) exactly 1?: This means the causal factor had zero impact. While possible, double-check that your “Factual” and “Counterfactual” datasets aren’t actually identical.

  • Is the FAR negative?: A negative Fraction of Attributable Risk suggests the factor reduced the risk. If you are studying human-induced warming and heatwaves, a negative FAR usually indicates a calculation error in the formula \(1 - (p_0 / p_1)\).

6.3 The “So What?” Test

Finally, evaluate the communicative value of your result. Ask yourself:

“If I tell a policymaker that an event is 1.2 times more likely, is that a meaningful change, or is it within the noise of natural variability?”

Attribution is most powerful when the results are distinct enough to drive action. If your results are borderline, consider if your threshold \(u\) is truly capturing an “extreme” or just a “heavy” event.
